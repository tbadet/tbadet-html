Research

Genome evolution in fungi

My research focuses on elucidating the evolutionary dynamics of microbial genomes, with particular emphasis on the impact of mobile genetic elements on genome architecture, function, and adaptability. Building on a foundation in plant pathology and further developed through evolutionary genomics, I employ a combination of experimental and computational methodologies to investigate the molecular interplay between mobile elements and genome defense systems. This integrative approach aims to advance our understanding of the mechanisms that govern microbial genome evolution and plasticity.

Which impact of genome defense / transposon interplay on genome architecture?

A key direction of my research concerns how transposon defense mechanisms influence genome architecture across fungi. Focusing on the kingdom-specific process Repeat-Induced Point mutation (RIP), I investigated how targeted mutagenesis of repetitive DNA shapes transposon activity and genome evolution in fungi, particularly within Ascomycota. Using large-scale comparative phylogenomics across more than a thousand fungal genomes, I reconstructed the evolutionary distribution of RIP-associated signatures and examined how changes in genome defense capacity correlate with genome architecture. This analysis showed that loss of RIP is strongly associated with large-scale genome expansion and increased genomic structural complexity, linking a molecular defense mechanism directly to long-term genome size evolution. Beyond characterizing RIP distribution, the work identified candidate molecular components potentially involved in generating RIP-like mutation patterns, including a zinc-finger protein with strong phylogenetic association signals. Overall, this research connects transposon control mechanisms with deep evolutionary shifts in genome structure, highlighting genome defense as a major determinant of fungal genome evolution.

Can structural variation be predicted from DNA sequence features?

Building on evidence that chromosomal rearrangements in the pathogen Zymoseptoria tritici are strongly associated with transposable element activity, I quantified the landscape of structural variation across the species pangenome and examined its evolutionary constraints. This analysis revealed that most classes of rearrangements are subject to strong purifying selection and are non-randomly distributed across the genome. To formalize the relationship between genome architecture and structural variation, I developed a predictive framework integrating sequence composition, recombination landscape, and transposable element density. The model identified these features as primary determinants of rearrangement probability and was validated on independently generated meiotic pedigrees. Beyond explaining existing variation, the approach demonstrated predictive power for newly generated structural variants, indicating that genome dynamics can be inferred from local genomic context. More broadly, this work establishes a link between genome sequence properties and the evolution of structural variation, providing a quantitative framework to study genome plasticity and its role in adaptation.

Can transposons promote chromosomal instability?

Transposable elements are strongly activated under starvation stress in the pathogen Zymoseptoria tritici. Here I investigated the evolutionary history and activity of a highly dynamic DNA transposon across the genus Zymoseptoria. By combining comparative genomics, population genomics, and experimental approaches, I examined how transposon activity varies across evolutionary and geographic scales and how genome defense mechanisms influence their persistence. This work revealed that transposable elements can remain evolutionarily active over long periods and continue to generate genetic variation within contemporary populations. Importantly, transposon insertions were closely associated with regions of genome restructuring, highlighting their role as drivers of chromosomal rearrangements and genome plasticity. More broadly, these findings illustrate how the interplay between transposable elements, recombination, and genome defense mechanisms shapes genome architecture and contributes to the evolutionary potential of fungal pathogens.

What is Zymoseptoria tritici pangenome diversity?

Despite its relatively recent emergence as a pathogen, Zymoseptoria tritici displays remarkable levels of genetic variation and extensive genome plasticity. To capture this diversity, I led the construction of a chromosome-scale pangenome spanning isolates sampled across the global distribution of the species. This work aimed to move beyond the limitations of single-reference genomes and provide a more comprehensive view of genome evolution within a species. The resulting pangenome revealed that a substantial fraction of the gene repertoire is variably present among individuals and associated with extensive structural variation. I'm currently assembling the species pangenome graph in order to leverage reference-free population-scale genomic frameworks to investigate evolutionary processes and trait variation.

Does codon optimisation support generalist lifestyle in fungal parasites?

A fundamental question in pathogen evolution is why do some fungal species infect hundreds of plant hosts, whereas closely related species remain highly specialized. While host specialization is often driven by gene-for-gene interactions between pathogen effectors and plant resistance genes, generalist pathogens rely more heavily on the secretion of large repertoires of digestive enzymes to colonize diverse hosts. Here we asked whether the evolution of broad host ranges is associated with genome-wide adaptations that support the metabolic demands of extensive protein secretion. By comparing genomes across a diverse set of fungal species, we explored how translational efficiency, codon usage, and protein evolution contribute to the emergence of generalist lifestyles. This work highlighted the role of selection acting beyond individual virulence genes, revealing how fundamental features of genome organization and gene expression can facilitate adaptation to a broad ecological niche.
